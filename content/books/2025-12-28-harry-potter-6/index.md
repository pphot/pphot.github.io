After the anger of book five, this one feels almost quiet. There's romance. There's mystery. Dumbledore teaches Harry about the past.

And then Dumbledore dies and you remember we've been marching toward war this whole time.

## Understanding the Enemy

The Pensieve memories are the heart of the book. Dumbledore isn't teaching Harry spells - he's teaching him to understand Voldemort.

Tom Riddle was brilliant, charming, handsome - and fundamentally broken. The orphanage memory where he's already hurting other children is chilling. He chose cruelty from the start.

Watching him create Horcruxes - splitting his soul seven times by murdering - shows the ultimate perversion of magic. He's so afraid of death that he destroys what makes life worth living.

## Harry and Ginny

Harry noticing Ginny feels earned. She's not pining anymore. She's confident, funny, talented. And suddenly Harry sees her.

The kiss after the Quidditch Cup victory is perfect - spontaneous, earned, witnessed by the entire common room.

The breakup to protect her breaks your heart even though you know it won't really keep her safe.

## The Half-Blood Prince

Harry finding a textbook full of magical shortcuts and using them to excel at Potions is such a good metaphor for taking advice from the wrong people.

The tips work. The Prince was brilliant. But Sectumsempra nearly kills Draco.

The reveal that Snape was the Prince adds another layer. Everything Harry learned came from the man he hates most.

## Dumbledore Falls

The tower scene is brutal. Dumbledore weakened from destroying the Horcrux. Draco can't kill him - he's not a murderer.

Then Snape arrives. And Snape kills Dumbledore.

The one person who knew the whole plan, who held everything together - gone.

The fake Horcrux with R.A.B.'s note is devastating. Regulus Black beat them to it. Dumbledore died for nothing.

_Rating: 9/10_
