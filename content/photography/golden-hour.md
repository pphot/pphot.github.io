There's something sacred about golden hour. That fleeting moment when the sun hangs low on the horizon, painting the world in warm amber and honey tones. Photographers call it the "magic hour," and once you've experienced it, you understand why.

I've been chasing golden hour for years now, across different continents and seasons. It's become something of an obsession – checking sunrise and sunset times before I even book accommodation, planning entire days around being in the right place when that light hits.

## Why Golden Hour Matters

The technical reason golden hour produces such beautiful light is simple: when the sun is low, its light travels through more of the atmosphere, scattering the blue wavelengths and leaving the warmer reds, oranges, and golds. The light is also softer and more directional, creating long shadows and dimensional depth that midday light can't match.

But the real reason golden hour matters has nothing to do with physics. It's about how it makes you feel. There's a warmth and gentleness to that light that seems to transform everything it touches. Ordinary scenes become extraordinary. Harsh edges soften. Colors deepen and glow from within.

It's a brief window – usually only 20-30 minutes of perfect light – which makes it precious. You can't be distracted or unprepared. You have to be present, ready, watching the light shift and change from minute to minute.

## The Technical Side

For those interested in the photography itself:

**Equipment:** Canon EOS R6, primarily with 24-70mm f/2.8 and occasionally 70-200mm f/2.8 for compression

**Settings:** I typically shoot in aperture priority mode (f/5.6 to f/11 depending on desired depth of field), letting the camera choose shutter speed. I often underexpose by 1/3 to 2/3 of a stop to preserve highlight detail and saturation.

**White Balance:** Usually set to "Cloudy" or "Shade" to enhance the warm tones rather than neutralize them. Golden hour is warm – embrace it.

**Post-Processing:** Minimal. Golden hour light is already so beautiful that heavy editing often ruins it. I typically adjust exposure slightly, add a touch of contrast, and sometimes increase vibrance (but never saturation – that way lies garish photos).

## The Waiting Game

The truth about golden hour photography is that it involves a lot of waiting. You arrive at your location early, scout compositions, set up, and then you wait. Sometimes the light is perfect. Sometimes clouds obscure it. Sometimes you discover your chosen spot faces the wrong direction.

But even on the days when the photos don't work out, there's something meditative about watching the light change. Sitting quietly as the world transitions from day to night (or night to day), noticing how the quality of light shifts every few minutes, how colors evolve and deepen.

I've met other photographers at golden hour spots – we nod to each other in silent camaraderie, all of us chasing the same ineffable thing. We share whispered tips about where the sun will set, warn each other about tourists about to walk through the shot, celebrate quietly when the light does something particularly spectacular.

## Why I Keep Chasing It

I could photograph golden hour forever and never get tired of it. Every time is different. Different weather, different clouds, different time of year, different place. The light is never quite the same twice.

But more than that, golden hour reminds me to pay attention. To be present. To notice beauty. In our busy lives, it's easy to miss the transition times, the in-between moments. Golden hour forces you to stop, to be still, to watch the world transform.

Photography is my excuse to do that. But really, I'm there for the light itself, for how it makes me feel, for the reminder that beauty is abundant and free and happens twice a day, every day, whether we're paying attention or not.

*"Light makes photography. Embrace light. Admire it. Love it. But above all, know light." – George Eastman*
